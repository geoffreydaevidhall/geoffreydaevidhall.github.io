I'm a Postdoctoral fellow in the philosophy department at the University of Notre Dame . You can view my cv here.

My primary research interests are in metaphysics and the philosophy of perception. In metaphysics my current focus has been on the metaphysics of propositions. My sympathies lie with the view that propositions form a Boolean algebra under the operations of conjunction and negation, and much of my recent work has been directed on developing and defending this view. I completed a dissertation titled The Algebraic Structure of Propositions at Notre Dame under the advisement of Jeff Speaks and Daniel Nolan exploring these topics. In the philosophy of perception I am primarily concerned with the metaphysics of phenomenal properties, though I am also working out some views on the epistemology of perception. Finally, I have additional interests in the philosohy of math and logic. Some of my recent research in these areas has focused on the nature of arbitrary objects, and the explanatory role of algebra and category theory.

Publications

  • "Indefinite Extensibility and the Principle of Sufficient Reason," Philosophical Studies, 178 (2): 471-492.2021. [abstract : preprint]

    • The principle of sufficient reason threatens modal collapse. Some have suggested that by appealing to the indefinite extensibility of contingent truth, the threat is neutralized. This paper argues that this is not so. If the indefinite extensibility of contingent truth is developed in an analogous fashion to the most promising models of the indefinite extensibility of the concept set, plausible principles permit the derivation of modal collapse.

Under Review

  • On propositional fineness of grain. [abstract : draft]

    • Booleanism is the thesis that the set of propositions forms a Boolean algebra under the operations of conjunction and negation. If Booleanism is true, then belief distributes over conjunction if and only if it is closed under entailment. Some authors have rejected Booleanism on this basis. I argue that we ought to reject the thesis that belief distributes over conjunction.

  • On the metaphysics of form. [abstract : draft]

    • Fine (2017) presents a puzzle of logical form and develops a solution based on his theory of arbitrary objects. This paper begins by reformulating Fine's puzzle as a "no-go theorem" in the context of abstract algebra. It then explores the space of theories of form in light of this puzzle. Two theories hold particular interest. One is naturally interpreted as a development of Fine's own theory. The other is novel, but bears an intimate connection to Fine's theory. The costs and benefits of each theory are explored.

  • On representation and the unity of the proposition. (R&R) [abstract : draft]

    • Propositions represent the entities from which they are formed. This fact has puzzled philosophers and some have put forward radical proposals in order to explain it. This paper develops a primivitist account of the representational properties of propositions that centers on the operation of application. As we will see, this theory wins out over its competitors on grounds of strength, systematicity and unifying power.

  • On phenomenal properties. [abstract : draft]

    • A property is phenomenal if and only if there is something that it is like to have it. A property is consistent if and only if it fails to entail every property. A property is luminous if and only if having that property entails knowing that you have it. I argue that phenomenal properties are all and only the consistent luminous properties.

  • On structure and the aim of belief. (R&R) [abstract : draft]

    • Recently several authors have argued that structure or joint-carvingness is an aim of belief. This paper argues that this is false. In particular, I argue that our theorizing about the relative naturalness of properties is not itself guided by relative naturalness.

In Preparation

  • On the unity of consciousness. [abstract]

    • This paper argues that the best explanation of the unity of consciousness is to be found in the principle that perceptual experience agglomerates: if one exes that p and one exes that q then one exes that p and q. The background metaphysics is intentionalist: the principle that perceptual experience is a propositional attitude. Several other intentionalist and non-intentionalist explanations are considered and argued against.

  • On whether 'phenomenally conscious' is vague. [abstract]

    • This paper defends the thesis that possibly something is such that it is borderline whether it is phenomenally conscious. Consciousness is vague. There are a number of arguments that purport to show the opposite view that consciousness is precise. I argue that these arguments depend on principles that are inconsistent with plausible, and somewhat standard, principles governing the notion of a borderline case.

  • On size and cardinality. [abstract]

    • Recently several authors have argued against the orthodox view that the size of a set is its cardinality. This paper addresses the question of the relation of size and cardinality from a category theoretic perspective. It is argued that while it is correct to conclude that Cantor's theorem has nothing in particular to do with size, there is nevertheless a plausible, broadly structuralist, justification for identifying size and cardinality.

  • On propositional and objectual attitudes. [abstract]

    • This paper defends and clarifies propositionalism: the the thesis that all attitudes are definable in terms of propositional attitudes.
