I'm a fifth year graduate student in the philosophy department at the University of Notre Dame . You can view my cv here.

My primary research interests are in metaphysics and the philosophy of mind. In metaphysics I have recently been interested in questions concerning how fine-grained propositions, properties and relations are. In my dissertation, I defend and explore the position Booleanism, which says, roughly, that propositions, properties and relations are no more fine-grained than classical logical equivalence. My current research in the philosophy of mind has concerned the metaphysics of consciousness.

I have additional interests in the philosophy of logic and math. One issue I have been thinking about recently is arbitrary object theory and the potential for bringing in category theoretic tools in developing it.

Publications

  • "Indefinite Extensibility and the Principle of Sufficient Reason," Philosophical Studies, Forthcoming. [abstract : preprint]

    • The principle of sufficient reason threatens modal collapse. Some have suggested that by appealing to the indefinite extensibility of contingent truth, the threat is neutralized. This paper argues that this is not so. If the indefinite extensibility of contingent truth is developed in an analogous fashion to the most promising models of the indefinite extensibility of the concept set, plausible principles permit the derivation of modal collapse.

Under Review

  • On the metaphysics of form. [abstract]

    • Fine (2017) presents a puzzle of logical form and develops a solution based on his theory of arbitrary objects. This paper begins by reformulating Fine's puzzle as a "no-go theorem" in the context of abstract algebra. It then explores the space of theories of form in light of this puzzle. Two theories hold particular interest. One is naturally interpreted as a development of Fine's own theory. The other is novel, but bears an intimate connection to Fine's theory. The costs and benefits of each theory are explored.

  • On representation and the unity of the proposition. [abstract]

    • Propositions represent the entities from which they are formed. This fact has puzzled philosophers and some have put forward radical proposals in order to explain it. This paper develops a primivitist account of the representational properties of propositions that centers on the operation of application. As we will see, this theory wins out over its competitors on grounds of strength, systematicity and unifying power.

  • On phenomenal properties. [abstract]

    • A property is phenomenal if and only if there is something that it is like to have it. A property is consistent if and only if it fails to entail every property. A property is luminous if and only if having that property entails knowing that you have it. I argue that phenomenal properties are all and only the consistent luminous properties.

    In Preperation

  • On the unity of consciousness. [abstract]

    • This paper argues that the best explanation of the unity of consciousness is to be found in the principle that perceptual experience agglomerates: if one exes that p and one exes that q then one exes that p and q. The background metaphysics is intentionalist: the principle that perceptual experience is a propositional attitude. Several other intentionalist and non-intentionalist explanations are considered and argued against.

  • On sensible properties and relations. [abstract]

    • Perceptual atomism is the view that the representation of sensible properties is more fundamental than the representation of sensible relations. Perceptual structuralism is the view that the reprsentation of sensible relations is more fundamental than the representation of sensible properties. Most authors have either adopted atomism or structuralism. This paper argues that the existence of phenomenal continua shows that both atomism and structuralism are false. The representation of phenomenal relations is modally independent of the representation of phenomenal properties.

  • On size and cardinality. [abstract]

    • Recently several authors have argued against the orthodox view that the size of a set is its cardinality. This paper addresses the question of the relation of size and cardinality from a category theoretic perspective. It is argued that while it is correct to conclude that Cantor's theorem has nothing in particular to do with size, there is nevertheless a plausible, broadly structuralist, justification for identifying size and cardinality.
